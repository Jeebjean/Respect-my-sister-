We’re asking everyone to dial down the heat a notch, and keep conversations civil — online and in person. It’s okay to be passionate about topics like COVID-19, but it’s not okay to personally attack others.

That’s a person you’re talking to.

Take a break from the heat.

Would you say that in person?

Remember that’s a real person you’re talking to on the other side of your comment or post. Imagine that person is sitting on a couch beside you. Would you talk to them in the same way in person?

You have more in common than not

Instead of focusing on your differences to others, find common ground and work out what you agree on and share with them — it’ll be more than you think.

Passionate, not personal

It’s totally okay to be passionate about things like COVID-19, but it’s not okay to make personal attacks on others for their views, and actually: it’s counter-productive because it doesn’t achieve anything. Re-read your comments and avoid using blanket judgements, too much use of the word “you” or insults.

Cool down before you fire up

Let’s leave the hot air to the summer. When it comes to comments on social media, take a breather to cool off before you post them. Taking a little break — even a short walk — then re-reading a comment before posting it can work wonders.

You can’t control or change people

Once you accept that you can't control or change people’s minds, life will be less stressful. Sure, you may care a lot about the decisions people make and how it could affect you. But trying to continuously convince people to believe what you do is exhausting. Hold and share your conviction and principles, but steer clear of trying to convince, change or control other people’s beliefs.

Words have power

It’s easy to forget while typing on a keyboard or a screen, but words have power — both good and bad. When words divide people, it's time to consider: Can the same point be made using language that keeps everyone involved in the conversation?

Acknowledge people's feelings

In general, we want people to understand our perspective and feelings instead of understanding theirs. While you don’t have to agree with other people’s beliefs, don’t assume you know their motivations. Since people make decisions based on multiple factors, don’t assume you know them all. Dismissing people’s feelings will likely make people feel unheard and unseen. It doesn’t take a lot to acknowledge that someone could be genuinely concerned, angry or fearful. An empathetic response to a family member or friend, could make the world of difference to someone.

Compassion is cool

No one likes a bully but compassion looks good on everyone. Show inclusion, compassion and respect for others and stand up for anyone who you think needs support. If you are worried about people who might be victims of online or offline vitriol, then make sure you reach out to them. Ask them how you can be of assistance if they require support.

Check your sources

Providing accurate information about people, events, and culture is important.  This is especially important when news reports have negative statements about any specific group. If you are unsure about the authenticity or accuracy of something you are about to share, perhaps try to verify it, or avoid sharing it as fact.

Responses when you don’t agree

A good way to avoid counterproductive debates that don’t go anywhere, is to rehearse a response ahead of time.  Here are some short and quick responses when you are feeling pressured to agree with someone, or if you do not wish to engage. Create your own response based on the following examples and save yourself energy.  

  • "thanks for sharing, that’s interesting"·
  • "that’s a lot of think about, I’ll keep it mind"·
  • "I’ve never heard that one before, I might do some research on my own"·
  • "let’s agree to disagree"·
  • "I respect your right to have a view. I choose to keep my views to myself"·
  • "I’ve made it a rule for myself to not get involved in these discussions"·
  • "I’d rather not talk about it, I hope you can respect that"·

If you have faced discrimination, hateful speech, or your human rights have been denied, contact the Human Rights Commission. Our team can help you to get the information and support you need from the commission or another organisation if they are better able to help.

What you are experiencing, or have experienced in the past, could be unlawful discrimination. If this is the case, you may be referred to our team of mediators for dispute resolution. They will step you through how we can work with you to help resolve your complaint.

Not all cases are discrimination under the Human Rights Act 1993, and not all situations will be referred for dispute resolution. Dispute resolution can also be available in cases of sexual or racial harassment. Learn more here or call the Human Rights Commission for free on 0800 496 877 or email infoline@hrc.co.nz.

More info about COVID-19 and Human rights

Even in times of crisis, people have human rights that safeguard their dignity. Even in times of emergency, human rights place binding obligations upon the Government to abide by commitments they have made. The Government has obligations to limit the spread of Covid-19, but restrictions must be necessary, proportionate and respectful of human dignity. The Government has Te Tiriti o Waitangi and human rights obligations to protect people’s economic and social rights, as well as their civil and political rights. The Human Rights Commission has been working to ensure these obligations are met.
